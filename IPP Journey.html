From speed-to-market to journey ownership

Adyen has offered In-Person Payments since 2017. The early journeys were simple — retail, terminals only — so documentation was skipped in favour of shipping fast.

Since then the offering has expanded massively: mobile in-person payments, a wide feature portfolio, and verticals like F&B, hospitality, and beauty & wellness — each with their own journeys. Despite that footprint, almost nothing is documented beyond a rudimentary Figma map. We never made the shift from speed-to-market to deeper product understanding.

What the gap costs us

No clear impact picture. Without journey thinking, we miss chances to streamline experiences for merchants and shoppers — and can't track performance, because we haven't defined what to measure.

No core experience metrics. Data is a one-off, often large lift. It frequently doesn't exist yet — devs must instrument new events and wait for a firmware release to populate it. Nothing is measured over time, so trends are invisible.

No single source of truth. Teams test flows just to learn how they behave, producing conflicting expectations of how the product should work. New feature journeys aren't assessed for impact, risking divergence from existing flows and missed parity or wow-moments — a real differentiator in-person.

Why this matters at our scale

  • 01See the whole experience landscape and how each new product or feature reshapes it.
  • 02Pinpoint what and where to measure to spot positive or negative impact and unlock opportunities.
  • 03Strengthen reliability and payment-flow tracking. Today no one fully knows the steps each payment goes through, or where shoppers and merchants hit pain.

Concrete example. P630 faults — a blank screen instead of the PresentCard screen — would be far easier to catch if the journeys were mapped and instrumented.

System context & documentation

Technical architecture, configuration hierarchy, and documentation references gathered from Hub and Adyen Docs — grounding the journey map in how the system actually works.

Configuration hierarchy

Settings inherit downward through 5 levels. Overrides at any level propagate to all children unless explicitly overridden further down.

PSP → Company → Merchant → Store → Terminal

73% of settings are overridden at the terminal level (the bottom). There is no single entry point for the settings application — merchants edit at account, store, or terminal level through different CA/BO paths.

Terminal Fleet Management (TFM)

Core database entities: tfmconfig (settings for an entity), tfmterminal (individual devices), store (merchant store), account (company/merchant/PSP), tfmprofile (config applied to terminals by filter).

Account Settings Profiles (GA Sep 2025) combine ADPs + TFM settings into reusable profiles for bulk configuration across accounts — reducing manual one-by-one setups.

EMV Kernel Configuration flow

The EMV config flow has 5 phases, triggered when a terminal boots or merchant settings change. This is the deepest layer of configuration — card-scheme-specific parameters that determine payment behaviour.

Supply chain journey

The IPP Platform Supply Chain team smooths the terminal's journey from warehouse to merchant. Services include:

  • •Dropshipping — Direct from warehouse to submerchant
  • •Custom packaging — Leaflets, QR codes, branded boxes for platform merchants
  • •Bulk shipping — Safety stock management (max 6 months in warehouse before firmware staleness)

Terminal boarding journey (from logs)

The terminal boarding sequence can be monitored via log queries combining these steps:

listStores→ getConfiguration→ selfBoarding→ merchant.json→ confirm→ maintenance

This sequence maps to journey steps 2.c → 3.c: the terminal receives its identity, downloads config, and enters operational state. Failures here create the support tickets that O3 and O4 aim to reduce.

Platform terminal onboarding — key friction insight

"Onboarding the terminal can be a source of friction and therefore impact the extent to which users fully adopt the solution. If the setup process is too difficult, users may drop off before they have fully adopted terminal payments."

— Adyen Docs, Platforms Quickstart Guide

Recommendations from docs (for AfP platforms)

  • 01Automate onboarding early. Start with operational support but focus on automating through a 'Terminal Manager' in the platform's customer environment — more scalable, better UX.
  • 02Define adoption benchmarks. Determine when a terminal is considered 'adopted' to monitor performance and identify improvement areas.
  • 03Maintain healthy safety stock. Low inventory risks out-of-stock; terminals in warehouse >6 months get stale firmware & low battery.
  • 04Consider software-first. Tap to Pay / Mobile SDK provides an easier starting point — less logistics, lower upfront investment. Move to hardware as users mature.
